Binnacle Store
Silence-Thunder

The times before the gyrocompass—tedious they were!
Stand by MUG count, aye aye, sir!
... because we're going on a field trip to the binnacle store.
A deviated compass is such an eyesore!

With corrector magnets and quadrantal spheres,
binnacles lessen how much the ship interferes.
You still must consult the deviation chart
for binnacles shield compasses only in part!

Binnies in the old days—their nails were made of iron,
but these days ferrous metals we no longer be desirin'.
If there is metal, it's likely nonmagnetic brass.
We could have plastic too or even fiberglass.

If ferrous metals interfere, a wise one recalls,
then why did Lord Kelvin put iron in the balls?
Symmetrically placed, they symmetricalize—
that means, strengthen magnetic fields to about identical highs.

From Latin habitāculum, meaning "little dwelling,"
come "habitacle" and "binnacle," and when the sea is swelling,
the gimbals keep the compass calm; indeed it's one good dwelling
for canaries and vines, as Herman Melville was telling!

Come down to the binnacle store, the pinnacle of reducing
magnetic interference that the ship's hull is producing
along with electrical equipment; that's why the flying bridge
is your binnie's own binnie, where deviation's just a smidge!
